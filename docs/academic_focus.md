# My Focus: Practical Engineering and Hard-Debugging

I wanted to write a brief, honest note on my background and what I care about. My GPA is pretty average, and I know that stands out in a bad way when applying to top-tier German universities. But the truth is, I have always struggled with traditional, theory-heavy exams. I get bored memorizing proofs just to reproduce them on paper. Where I actually feel alive is in the lab, staring at a terminal, trying to figure out why my CUDA kernels are throwing out-of-memory errors or why my PyTorch tensor hooks are leaking memory.

With AutoWeld-Vision, I didn't want to just be a "wrapper developer" who imports high-level anomaly libraries like Anomalib, adjusts a YAML config, and calls it research. I wanted to understand what is happening under the hood. So, I wrote the core models from scratch. 

It was a messy process, and I hit a lot of walls. For example, my initial implementation of the greedy minimax coreset selection for PatchCore ran extremely slow. A naïve double-loop distance calculation over tens of thousands of feature patches completely choked my local GPU memory. I had to learn how to express the L2 norm calculations using expanded matrix operations (the old standard $a^2 + b^2 - 2ab$ trick) to compute all distances in parallel, and I quickly realized I had to hard-cap the coreset size at 1500 patches to keep inference latency under 40 milliseconds on cheap edge hardware. 

Another huge headache was the late-fusion step. If you combine raw distance scores from PatchCore and a student-teacher network, you get garbage because their scales are totally different. I had to write a Platt Scaling calibrator to turn those arbitrary Euclidean distances into calibrated probabilities in $[0, 1]$. The Nelder-Mead optimization would constantly diverge at the start until I figured out how to initialize the scaling slope and bias based on class statistics and implement a numerically stable log-likelihood calculation using `numpy.log1p` to prevent floating-point underflows. 

I also spent a lot of time writing the simulated Modbus socket and the local tamper-evident audit trail. In my first test, a slow network response from my mock PLC completely locked up the OpenCV camera frame-reader, causing the entire video feed to freeze. I had to refactor the socket connection with a strict 50ms timeout so the main inference loop could drop the frame-transmission and keep going if the receiving PLC device was offline. 

This project isn't perfect, and my student-teacher network didn't converge as well as I hoped (which I discuss honestly in my reports). But this codebase is an honest record of my engineering values: I write my own PyTorch code, I debug my own runtime bottlenecks, and I care about building systems that actually work in the real world.
